Terbium (Tb)

Element Name:
Terbium (Tb) is a chemical element belonging to the lanthanide series, a group of rare earth metals. These metals, despite their name, are not particularly scarce in the Earth's crust.
Atomic Mass:
158.92534 u
The atomic mass represents the average weight of all isotopes of an element, taking into account their natural abundance. The value of 158.92534 u indicates that terbium is one of the heavier rare earth elements.
Atomic Number:
65
The atomic number indicates the number of protons in the nucleus of a terbium atom. This places terbium in the f-block of the periodic table, among the lanthanide series metals.
Number of Isotopes:
Terbium has 36 known isotopes, but only Tb-159 is stable. An isotope is a variant of an element that has the same number of protons but a different number of neutrons. Radioactive isotopes of terbium are used in various scientific applications.

This temperature indicates the point at which terbium transitions from a solid to a liquid state. This relatively high melting point is characteristic of many rare earth metals.

3230 ¬∞C (5846 ¬∞F)
The boiling point represents the temperature at which terbium transitions from a liquid to a gaseous state. This high value indicates that terbium remains liquid over a wide temperature range, useful in high-temperature applications.
Density:
8.27 g/cm¬≥
The density of terbium is moderate compared to other metals. This property is important when considering its use in alloys and other materials.
Electronegativity:
1.2 (Pauling scale)
Electronegativity measures the ability of an atom to attract electrons in a chemical bond. The low value of 1.2 indicates that terbium is electropositive and tends to lose electrons in chemical reactions.
Year of Discovery:
1843, Carl Gustaf Mosander
Terbium was discovered by the Swedish chemist Carl Gustaf Mosander while studying yttria, a mineral containing several rare earth elements. Its discovery contributed significantly to the understanding of this group of elements.
Abundance:
Terbium is relatively rare, with an abundance of about 1.2 parts per million (ppm) in the Earth's crust. It is found in minerals such as monazite and xenotime, often occurring alongside other rare earth elements.
Characteristics:
  • Silvery-white, lustrous, and soft metal, making it relatively easy to work with.
  • Belongs to the lanthanide series of the periodic table.
  • Fairly stable in air, although it slowly oxidizes to form a protective oxide layer.
  • Reacts with acids to form salts.
  • Possesses unique magnetic properties, making it valuable in advanced magnetic materials.
Technological Applications:
  • Screens and Displays: Used in phosphors for color displays, particularly in green phosphors, enhancing color purity and brightness.
  • High-Performance Magnets: An essential component in neodymium magnets (NdFeB), improving their performance and stability, crucial for electric vehicles, wind turbines, and other high-tech applications.
  • Lasers and Optical Devices: Used in certain types of lasers and optical materials.
  • Nuclear Reactors: Has applications in some nuclear technologies.
  • Ecological Devices: Important in the development of sustainable technologies, such as in certain types of catalytic converters and in materials for clean energy generation.
Location of Deposits:
  • China: Inner Mongolia and the Bayan Obo mine are significant sources.
  • USA: The Mountain Pass mine in California is a notable deposit.
  • Australia: Deposits at Mount Weld are among the richest outside of China.
  • Sweden: A significant deposit was recently discovered.
  • Africa: South Africa and Madagascar have reserves that are less exploited.
Health Effects:
Terbium is considered slightly toxic if ingested and can be an irritant to the skin and eyes upon direct contact. Precautions should be taken when handling terbium compounds.
Environmental Effects:
Terbium itself does not pose a significant environmental threat under normal conditions. However, the extraction and processing of minerals containing terbium can have negative environmental impacts. These processes often involve the use of toxic substances and can generate radioactive waste, potentially contaminating the surrounding soil and water.
Where is it used in phones?
  • Screen Phosphors: Terbium compounds are used in the green phosphors of smartphone screens to enhance color quality and brightness.
  • Vibrating Motors: Terbium, along with dysprosium, is used in the magnets of the tiny vibrating motors in phones, providing efficient and compact vibration.
  • Speakers and Microphones: The powerful magnets made with neodymium, iron, and boron (NdFeB), which often contain terbium to improve performance at higher temperatures, are used in the speakers and microphones of smartphones for clear sound quality.
